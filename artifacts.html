ARTIFACT WALL

Five objects that changed portable listening

Each artifact includes what it is, why it matters, and a source visitors can use to learn more.

1979

Sony Walkman TPS-L2

What it is: Sony’s first Walkman model, widely recognized as the device that helped define portable personal listening.

Why it matters: It changed music from a shared room experience into an individual mobile experience.

Smithsonian object page
1984

Sony Discman D-50

What it is: One of the earliest portable CD players, originally sold in the United States as the Discman D-50.

Why it matters: It moved portable audio into the compact disc era and shaped expectations around digital sound and track control.

Sony historical reference
1995

MP3 format

What it is: A compressed audio format whose name was selected in 1995 and that helped music circulate as portable digital files.

Why it matters: It made large music libraries easier to store, share, and carry.

Fraunhofer history page
2001

Apple iPod

What it is: Apple’s pocket-sized music player introduced as a device that could hold up to 1,000 CD-quality songs.

Why it matters: It made the digital music library itself portable and iconic.

Apple launch announcement
2008

Spotify

What it is: A music streaming platform that shifted listening from owned files to on-demand access.

Why it matters: It changed the portable music model from collection to connection.

Spotify company overview
